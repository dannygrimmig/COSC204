Critical Reasoning

Formal Logic is concerned with the form or structure of argument

What does it meant to be logical?

Critical reasoning gives us the tools to clarify our own thinking, analyze the arguments of others, and persuade others to consider our line of reasoning.

An Argument (formally defined)

Informal: An argument refers to a disagreement or verbal altercation

Formal: An argument is simply a reasoning form or structure that consists of several claims

Example Argument

  • Premise 1: It is legal to write a book on how to build a bomb
  • Premise 2: Writing a book is ismilar to writing a blog post
  • Conclusion: It should be legal to write a blog post on how to build a bomb

Invalid: Premise 2 using the word similar invalidates it

Because the conclusion is a normative claim, we must use the form of reasoning

Valid vs. Invalid Arguments

Arguments are not true or false, they are only valid or invalid

  • To determine validity, we ask:
    • Is there a relationship between the premises and the conclusion such that if all of the premises in the argument are assumed true, it would be impossible for the conclusion ot be false?
  • It is possible for all the claims of an argument (premises and conclusion) to be objectively true, but for the argument to be invalid becausethe premises do not support the conclusion.

Counterexamples

To show an argument is invalid, produce a counter example:

Example 1

  • Premise 1: All dogs like sticks
  • Premise 2: Poodles are dogs
  • Comclusion: Poodles like sticks

Valid: No true or false, while this might be false it is still a valid claim.

Sound vs. Unsound Arguments

If an argument is valid, then the next question is whether it is sound

Question 1: True

Question 2: Unsure

Example 3

  • Premise 1: 90% of Colgate students own an article of maroon clothing
  • Premise 2: John is a colgate student
  • Conclusion: John has an article of maroon clothing

Inductive vs. Fallacious Arguments

  • Inductive
    • If an argument is invalid, but the conclusion is still very likely to be true (when we assume the truth of both premises), the the argument is inductive
  • Fallacious
    • If an argument is invalid, but the conclusion is unlikely to be true (when we assume the truth of both premises), the the argument is fallacious

Both inductive and sound arguments are considered strong

Can you have an argument that is invalid, but still strong?

  • Yes!

Informal Logical Fallacies

Ad Hominem: The argument attacks someone's character, using poor character as evidence for conclusion, rather than focusing on substance of debated topic

  • Most common informal fallacy is in politics

Sometimes, a persons character can be debated however in relevence to the debate topic.

Slippery Slope: The argument assumes C will follow B, and B will follow from A, etc, without providing for clear evidence for this causal chain

  • Ex: "If we allow a man to marry a man, what is to stop a main from marrying a goat"
  • It is ok to make causual chain, only when it is actually relevent (unlike above)

Informal Logical Fallacies

Appeal to Authority: Argument relies on an expert or authority figure's connclusion, even though that expert may not have relevent knowledge on the topic in question

  • Ex: Ask Fauci about medical advice, but not advice on march madness bracket

Appeal to Emotion: Argument that relies on an emotional response rather than evidence

  • Emotions are an important part of argumentation though (pathos always beats logos)

False Cause/Post Hoc Egrgo Propter Hoc: B happened after A, therefor A caused B

Correlation vs. Causation: A is associated with B, therefore A causes B

  • Ex/ video games causes you to not afford a house... not relevent

Equivocation: Equating two different definitions of the same word. Or sometimes redefining the word in question to suit your needs.

False Dichotomy: Presenting an either or choice, when in actuality there are more than two options

Strawman: Mischaracterizing your opponents position and then attacking that position instead of the actual one they had

Freedom of speech is a right, so it is only right that we speak out against vegetarians who want to force us to only ever eat carrots. The choice is clear, freedom or tyranny.

  • Strawman
  • False Dichotomy

Key Takeaways

Validity has to do with the structure or form of an argument

Soundness has to do with the truth of the premises of a valid argument

Inductive arguments are invalid arguments where the conclusion is likely true (assuming the truth of the premises)

Inductive and sound arguments are both considered strong
