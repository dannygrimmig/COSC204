Are These Violations of First Amendment?

Not Violations

  • A private school suspends a student for criticizing a school policy
  • A private business fires an employee for expressing political views on the job
  • A private media company refuses to publish a book based on views

Violations

  • A person blackmails another person
  • A person plagariszes another person

Unprotected Categories of Speech

  • Child Pornography
  • Some commercial speach (false advertizing, advertising drugs/gambling to minors
  • Blackmail
  • Defamation
  • Fighting Words (very utterance inflict injury, incite breach peace)
  • Incite immediate lawless action
  • National Security
  • Obsecenity ("You know it when you see it")
  • Perjury (lying under oath)
  • Plagarism
  • Solicitations to commit crimes

U.S Laws aim to be General and Ideology-Neutral

ex/ A racist is legally allowed to be racist and engage in racist speech (as long as it does not cause physical harm

Other Countries are more specific, less neutral

  • ex/ Germany does not allow Pro Natzi speech (twitter)

We sa individuals do not have to be ideology-neutral

It may be useful to distinguish between the legal standard and the moral standard

Some argue that adhering to an ideology-neutral stance as individuals gives extremist ideologies (ex/ white nationalism) an advantage in online discourse

Special Categories

Speech rights can be more limited for:

  • K-12 Public School Students: If speech substantially disrupts shool environment or invades others rights
  • Government Employees: Speech mahy be protected if the speech is a matter of public concern
  • People in State/Federal Prison: Speech can be regulated if it is reasonably related to a legitamate and neutral interest of the prison

Regulation: What

In physical spaces, regulations often focus on content and process

Regulating certain kinds of speech would be an example of content regulation

Regulating what kinds of data websites can collect would be process regulation

Regulation: How

Lawrence Lessig's Four Modalities of Regulation

  1. Laws (is it explicitly legal/illegal)
  2. Social Norms (is it socially acceptable/unacceptable)
  3. Market Pressures (do consumers want/not want it
  4. Code/Architecture (is it encouraged/discouraged structurrally or systemically)

Key Takeaways

Freedom of Speech is freedom from government restrictions on speech

Still many exceptions, special circumstances that permit government regulations of speech

Private businesses can restric speech
