Ethical Theories

Provide a framework for analyzing moral issues

  • Internally coherent, consistent, comprehensive, and systematic

Four Standard Types of Ethical Theories

  1. Consequence Based
  2. Duty Based
  3. Contract Based
  4. Character Based

Consequence Based Ethical Theories

Hold that consequences of actions and policies (the ends) are ultimate standard for evaluating moral decisions

  • If you have to choose between A and B, pick which has most desirable outcome
  • But... most desirable outcome for whom?

Utiltarianism: Consequence Based Ethical Theories

Utilitarians argue that you should maximize the best outcome for the greatest number of individuals.

Act (X) or policy (Y) is morally permissible if its consequences produce greates good for greatest amount of people

Famout Utilitarians

Jeremy Bentham defended the Utilitarian ethical theory with two claims:

  1. Social utility is superior to alternative criteria for evaluating moral systems
  2. Social utility can be measured by the amount of happiness produced

John Stuart Mill argued that happiness is an intrinsic good> because people desire it not as a means to one or more ends, but for its own sake.

Trolly Problem

If a driverless car causes injry or death, who is responsible?

Act vs Rule Utilitarianism

Act Utilitarianism claims that:

  • An act (X) is morally permissible if the consequences produced by doing X result in the greatest good for the greatest number of persons affected by X

Rule Utilitarianism claims that:

  • An act (X), is morally permissible if the consequences of following teh general rule (Y), of which act X is an instance, would bring greatest good for the greatest number

Problems with Utilitarianism

Critics tend to attack two features:

  • Morality is tied to the production of happiness
  • Morality can ultimately be decided by consequences (of either acts or policies)

Argue that morality can't be grounded in consequences or happiness

Duty Based Ethical Theories

Immanual Kant argued that moralitiy is grounded in duty, or the obligations that humans have to one another

  • Rejects the notion that morality is tied to the consequences of actions or the promotion of happiness

Deontological Theories are theories where the notion of duty or obligation serves as the foundation for morality

Rule Deontology

Categorical Imperative is a rule (or variations on rules) that can be used as an objective and impartial way for determining the basis of our moral oblications

  1. Always act on a rule that ensures that all individuals will be treated as ends in themselves and not as means to an end
  2. Always act on a rule that can be universally binding, without exception, for all human beings (Universality)

Problems with Rule Deontology

What if we hae two or more conflicting duties?

Is it wrong to steal bread to feed your children?

  • It is wront to steal
  • It is wrong to allow your children to starve

It is not clear how we should navigate these conflicts under Kant's Deontology

Act Deontology

David Ross proposes an Act Deontology that weighs the context of each competing prima facie duties (self evident duties)

  • Still rejects the notion that consequences should determine the relative morality of a choice
  • Instead, uses the specific contet of a dilemma to clarify further action

Relies on rational intuition

Key Takeaways

Utilitarianism focuses on maximizing the most good, either emphasizing teh acts themselves or the general rules under which specific actions fall

Deontology grounds morality in our duty (to respect one another as ends in and of themselves -- and never as means to an end
